Introduction


Ludwig Mies van der Rohe was a German-American architect, academic, and interior designer. Referred to as Mies in design circles, he is regarded as one of the pioneers of modern architecture. Seeking to establish his own particular architectural style that would exude modernity, his buildings made use of modern materials such as industrial steel and plate glass to define interior spaces. This aesthetic would influence furniture design, product design, typefaces and the visual arts. He is often associated with his fondness for the aphorisms "less is more" and "God is in the details". Gaining the accolades of celebrated peers like Frank Lloyd Wright, Mies occupies a vaunted place within modern architecture as one of the founders of the International Style in Germany. He helped establish the International Style as the definitive architectural language of North American post-war modernism and influenced hundreds of emulators worldwide. His steel-and-glass aesthetic became the archetype of the term "modern architecture" for decades even after his death.

Early Life

  • 1886 - Born Maria Ludwig Michael Mies in Aachen, Kingdom of Prussia, German Empire.
  • 1905 - Moved to Berlin at 19, after manifesting talent in draughtsmanship during apprenticeships at several local ateliers and architectural offices.
  • 1907 - Independently completed his first project during his service with the designer Bruno Paul, with whom he worked from 1906 until 1908. This was a private house in Potsdam-Neubabelsberg commissioned by Alois Riehl.

In Berlin with Behrens

  • 1908-11 - Worked as an assistant, with Walter Gropius and Le Corbusier, in the office of Peter Behrens, probably the most important living German architect at the time. Behrens was widely known for his commercial work in Berlin, but he also designed residences that demonstrated the new regard for classicism.
  • 1911-14 - Opened a private practice in Berlin.
  • 1913 - Married Ada Bruhn, a wealthy woman who bore him three daughters between 1914 and 1917. Thereafter, he and his family lived largely on her money, leaving him free to become familiar with the Berlin artistic avant-garde and foster friendships with its more energetic members. They separated in 1921.
  • 1919 - Submitted the Kroeller-Mueller project to an exhibition of younger architects supervised by Walter Gropius, who was a determined and accomplished devotee of the modernist point of view. Mies' entry was rejected.
  • 1919-37 - Continued in private practice.

Development of Design Identity

  • 1921 - Changed his name to Ludwig Mies van der Rohe to incorporate his mother's heritage.
  • 1921 - By this time, he had embraced the clear-span pavilion, which he believed facilitated the accommodation of varying uses over time.
  • 1921-25 - As Director of the architectural division in the Novembergruppe, he conceived the design of five projects that, although never built, would affect the mainstream history of 20th-century architecture. He showed models or drawings of several of these works in exhibitions, attracting the attention of the Berlin art world.

Growth at the Deutscher Werkbund

  • Mid-1920s - He had assumed a place of importance in several of Berlin’s professional architectural organizations. He helped finance a journal, G (for “Gestaltung”), which stood for the new elementarist position, and contributed several manifesto-like articles.
  • 1925 - Mies’ work had become steadfastly modern in style, for the most part rectilinear in its contours, cubic in its massing, and open in plan whenever possible. He retained a distinctly personal fondness for brick, a material closer to the crafts tradition than to the machine aesthetic he claimed to embrace in his writings of the period.
  • 1925 - Founding member of Zehner Ring (later Der Ring), an architectural collective with a functionalist agenda,as opposed to Historicism.
  • 1926-32 - Promoted to vice president at the Deutscher Werkbund, Berlin.
  • 1927 - Collaborated on exhibition projects with Lilly Reich; director of Werkbund exhibition Weissenhofsiedlung, Stuttgart.

Bauhaus and Emigration

  • 1930 - Appointed director of the Bauhaus at the recommendation of Walter Gropius. Mies accepted this directorship in Dessau, where the school remained until local right-wing political elements forced its relocation to Steglitz and subsequent closure in 1933. Shortly thereafter, he reopened it as a private school in Berlin.
  • 1931 - Became a member of the Prussian Academy of Arts and Sciences.
  • 1931 - Appointed director of the Werkbund section “The Dwelling” at the Berlin Building Exhibition.
  • 1938 - Emigrated to the United States and settled in Chicago, where he opened a private practice.
  • 1938 - Assumed the headship of the School of Architecture at the Armour Institute of Technology, which later became the Illinois Institute of Technology (IIT). He held this post until 1959. He spent the last 30 years of his life in the United States, during which he built more actively but went through fewer stylistic shifts.

Chicago School

  • 1939-40 - His first major commission in the United States was a master plan for the campus of IIT. The plan went through a number of phases before construction began.
  • 1944 - Mies became a naturalised citizen of the United States.
  • 1951 - Farnsworth House, the realisation of his clear-span pavilion. A quintessential American Mies design, it features a floor slab connected to a roof slab by wide-flanged columns. This design was repeated in many of his American high-rises, usually with a curtain wall of similar form over the main frame.
  • 1958 - His masterpiece, the Seagram Building (1958) in New York, is widely recognized for its lavish design and use of bronze cladding and travertine in the foyer. It solidified Mies’ influence on corporate-scaled architecture in the United States in the 1950s and 1960s.

Awards and Final Years

  • 1959 - He was elected President of the Congrès Internationaux d'Architecture Moderne (CIAM) while receiving a Royal Gold Medal from the Royal Institute of British Architects.
  • 1959 - He would then receive a Commander's Cross of the Order of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany, along with the prestigious Pour le Mérite.
  • 1960 - He attained the AIA Gold Medal from the American Institute of Architects.
  • 1963 - He became a member of the National Institute of Arts and Letters.
  • 1963 - Awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom.
  • 1969 - Died in Chicago, USA, at the age of 83.

Legacy


Mies’ contribution to the modern urban landscape is inescapable, whether or not one agrees with his assertion that less is more. In the same way that Gothic was expressive of the age of ecclesiasticism, so was his International Style expressive of the industrial age. Mies’ designs with their modular language endow our built environment with significance and beauty, rather than mere objects to be lived in. They radiate confidence, rationality, and elegance of their creator and, confess the essential elements of our lives. These indeed are not trivial or sterile. In our time, where excess prevails, this reductionist approach is as pertinent as ever. As we reduce the distractions and focus on the essential elements of our environment and ourselves, we find that they are great, intricate, and beautiful. Less, indeed, is more.

"Architecture depends on its time. It is the crystallization of its inner structure, the slow unfolding of its form."

- Ludwig Mies van der Rohe
